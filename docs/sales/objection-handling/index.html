Objection Handling

Sales

7 documented objections with rebuttals. Empathize first, probe second, rebuttal third. Every call ends with at least a tentative reservation.

⚓
Universal Fallback — Use Every Time

If you can't close the sale, never let the call end with nothing. Always offer a tentative reservation — free, no commitment, fully changeable. It keeps Sparrow on the calendar.

Step 1 — Probe First

Ask before launching into a rebuttal. Their answer changes your response entirely.

Probe "I totally get it. How many estimates do you have so far? And when it comes down to it, what's your top priority — price, service, or getting the right date?"

By Priority

If Price Is Priority "The lowest price isn't always the most cost-effective. Send me that estimate and we'll look at it together. If it's legitimate and the same services, we'll do our best to beat it."
If Service Is Priority "Our movers are licensed, insured, and top-rated. We're here 7 days a week. You'd be hard-pressed to find a company as dedicated to your satisfaction as ours."
If Date Is Priority "Even without a final decision, we can lock in your date right now at no risk. Dates can change anytime — but availability goes fast."
💡 Always close with the tentative reservation if they're still undecided.

Step 1 — Probe the Quote

Most "lower quotes" aren't actually apples-to-apples.

Probe "Is their quote hourly or flat rate? And how many guys are on the crew?"
If It's a 2-Man Crew "A 3-man crew will actually save you money. One loads while two carry — your billable hours drop significantly. In apartment buildings, one holds the elevator while two work. That efficiency more than offsets the higher hourly rate."
Review the Quote Together "Send me their estimate — I want to make sure you're comparing the same services. Staffing surcharges, transportation surcharges, professional quilt-wrapping — these are often hidden costs that won't show up in the base rate."
Manager Discount Close "Let me talk to my manager about an additional 10% discount. That brings us down to [adjusted rate]. If I can lock that in, can we get you set up today?"

Walk Through the Hidden Costs

Most customers haven't done this math. Walk through it with them.

  • Truck rental fee
  • Insurance (rental companies charge extra for moving)
  • Fuel — miles ÷ 8 mpg × current gas price
  • Moving blankets, dollies, equipment rental
  • Packing materials
  • Physical labor — your time, their back, friends' time
  • Risk to valuables — no professional wrapping, no coverage
  • For long-distance: add lodging costs
The Close "Once you factor everything in, hiring a professional is often cheaper — and that's before you account for your time and the risk to everything you own. We guarantee our work. A rental truck doesn't."
First Response "Totally understand — and I want you both comfortable. Here's the thing: our availability is limited. Let's set up a no-risk reservation right now. The deposit is fully refundable. Your spouse can review everything, and if they say no, just call us back — no questions asked."
If They Still Resist "How long do you need to talk it over?" [Let them answer.] "Perfect — I'll put you on a tentative reservation in the meantime so you don't lose the date."
💡 Never argue with "I need to talk to my spouse." Empathize and redirect to protecting the date.
Rebuttal "Most of our customers in that situation still book in advance to lock in their current rate — good for up to 2 years. Dates can change anytime at no extra charge. There's nothing to lose, and you'll have peace of mind knowing your crew and truck are already secured."
💡 This objection is almost always a win if you present it right. The 2-year rate lock is a genuine differentiator.

Process

  • Pull up their account, confirm identity, make small talk first
  • Don't react immediately — build a moment of rapport
Step 1 — Ask Why "I'm sorry to hear that. Can I ask what made you decide to cancel?"
If Going to a Competitor or DIY Use the "Lower Quote" or "Renting a Truck" rebuttal above based on their answer.
If Move Is Delayed (No New Date) "No problem — I can place the move on hold rather than canceling. That locks in your current rate in case our prices go up before you're ready."
If They Insist on Full Cancellation "Your refund will process within the hour. Please don't hesitate to reach out when the time is right — we'd love to earn your business."

This is a proactive outbound call when you sense a customer may be about to cancel. Same script as Objection 6 — key difference is the tone.

Opening — Curious, Not Defensive "Hey [name], I was just going over your file and wanted to reach out. Everything still on track for [date]?"

Let them respond. If there's hesitation, move into the Objection 6 process — probe the reason, then redirect to a hold or the appropriate rebuttal.

The goal of this call is to surface the real concern before they've made a firm decision. It's much easier to save a booking at this stage than after they've already called to cancel.

Key Principles

1

Empathize first. Every rebuttal starts with "I totally understand" or "That makes sense." Never argue.

2

Probe before you rebuttal. Ask what their concern actually is. The answer changes your response.

3

Always offer a path forward. Discount, hold, review together — never leave them with a dead end.

4

The tentative reservation is always the fallback. Never let a call end with nothing.

5

Use urgency responsibly. Limited availability, price increases, fuel costs — these are real. Don't manufacture pressure, but don't hide it either.

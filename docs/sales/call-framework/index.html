9-Step Call Framework

Sales

80% scripted, 20% spontaneous. Follow the steps on every call — every customer gets the same experience, nothing gets missed. Calls run 20–40 minutes when done well.

The Reset

  • Stand up. Walk a few steps — energy travels through your voice.
  • If the last call went poorly, leave it there. The next customer didn't cause it.
  • Remind yourself: this person is going through a major life transition. Bring your best.
⚠️ Pitfall: Stacking calls without resetting. A bad call leaks into the next one. The fix is physical — stand up, walk, breathe.

Opening

The Opener "Thanks for calling Sparrow Moving, this is [Name]. I know you're reaching out about a move — we're going to take care of you. But first, what has you moving today?"

Listen to the answer. This question almost always surfaces something real — new job, first home, baby on the way, downsizing after a parent passed, divorce. React genuinely.

If It's Good News "Congratulations — that's huge. Let's make sure this part is one less thing on your plate."
If It's Hard News "I'm really sorry to hear that. We'll do everything we can to make this part smoother for you."

Ask One More Question

The Follow-Up "What else is going on around the move that we should know about?"

This surfaces the surrounding life: work travel, kids' first day of school, a partner recovering from surgery. It opens the door for better questions and makes Step 4 hit harder.

The Pain Funnel — When to Drill

Any time the customer mentions a stressor, don't acknowledge-and-pivot. Drill 1–2 layers deeper before moving on. Takes 30 seconds; changes how the rest of the call lands.

Technique: "Tell me more about that" → "Why is that important?" — in your own words.

Example — Past Bad Mover Customer: "We had a bad experience with movers last time."
You: "I'm sorry — what happened?"
Customer: "They broke our dining table."
You: "Ugh. Was it a piece that mattered to you?"
Customer: "It was my wife's grandmother's."

You now know exactly what to anchor on in Step 4. Honor it.
Example — Tight Timing Customer: "We're moving the weekend before my wife's first day at her new job."
You: "Tight window — what happens if move day goes sideways?"
Customer: "She'd be unpacking until midnight Sunday and walking into Monday wrecked."

Premium pricing isn't a defense anymore. It's protection of her first day.
⚠️ Pitfall: The pain funnel is curiosity, not interrogation. Would a friend ask this if you mentioned your moving stress? If yes, you're on-brand. If it feels like extracting information, dial back.

Read the Personality — First 30 Seconds

TypeHow to Adjust
Storyteller — elaborates, friendly, shares context Match warmth, react genuinely, let them lead the rapport
Straight Shooter — clipped, just-the-facts "You sound like a straight shooter — let's get right to it. I just need an inventory and I'll get you a number. Sound good?"

The process is identical either way. The framing changes everything.

Build Value Before Logistics

If They've Never Used a Professional Mover "Let me tell you what to expect from us. We pad-wrap all of your furniture. We put down floor runners and door jamb protectors at both locations — your house gets protected from the start. We break down anything that needs it and reassemble at the destination. None of that costs extra. We treat your stuff and your home the way we'd treat our own."
If They've Had a Bad Experience "I'm really sorry — that happens more than it should in this industry. Just so you know how we work: our guys are full-time, trained on our own program, and protecting your stuff is genuinely the job. Let me walk you through what to expect."
💡 Don't trash the previous mover. Lead with what we do, never what they did wrong.
Transition Line "Let me get you loaded in so I can build out your quote."

Enter into Chariot

  • Name — ask spelling on unusual last names
  • Phone, email
  • Move date — "Do you have a date you're targeting, or if not, a time of month you're planning for?"
  • Service type — moving only vs. packing + moving
  • Origin address + property type, parking, floor, carry distance
  • Destination address + property type, parking, carry distance
  • Lead source — always ask, always enter. No exceptions.
💡 If Step 1 went well, almost everything you need is already known. Confirm, don't re-ask. "I have you at a first-floor apartment moving to a single-family home — let me get you loaded in." Confirmation feels organized. Re-asking feels chaotic.

💡 A customer without a firm date isn't a problem. Get a tentative date based on the time of month they mention. Don't let a missing date stall the call.

You Name the Items — They Confirm

The Right Way "Let's start with the master. Do you have a bed in there? What size — queen, king? Box spring? Any nightstands? Dresser — wide or tall chest? Need us to handle disassembly on any of that?"
Never ask: "What do you have in the bedroom?" — customers freeze on open questions. You name items, they confirm yes or no.

Room Order — Non-Negotiable

  • Phase 1: All bedrooms — master first, then each additional
  • Phase 2: Primary rooms — living room, dining room, kitchen
  • Phase 3: Catch-all — "Are there any spaces we haven't gone through yet? Office, sunroom, garage, shed, patio storage?"

Phase 3 is where forgotten items live. The garage question alone regularly surfaces half a truck of stuff.

Confirm Each Room Before Moving On

Room Confirmation "Okay — so in the master we've got: king bed, frame, two nightstands, dresser. That's everything? And when we show up, all the boxes are packed and ready — we're just handling the furniture. Correct?"

That second sentence sets a move-day expectation and gets an early "correct."

If They Go Off Script "I want to make sure I don't miss anything — can we go room by room? We'll get to that after."

Box Count Estimate

After inventory is complete: ~10 boxes per 1,000 lbs, 1 wardrobe box per 1,000 lbs. Use the Pack Calculator for a full breakdown when quoting packing services.

💡 Specificity is what separates a great estimate from one that blows up on move day. Go room by room, every time.

Translate the Numbers

Weight-to-Story Script "Okay — based on what we walked through, you're at about [X] pounds of estimated inventory. That probably means nothing to you, so let me tell you what it means to me. A fully loaded truck is around 8,000 pounds. At [X], you're [a small move / a solid full load / a really big move]. Based on the inventory, we're sending [crew] and you're looking at around [Y] hours."

Proactively Describe the Service

Say all of this before they ask:

Standard Service Description "Here's what I want you to know about how we work. We pad-wrap all of your furniture. Floor runners and door jamb protectors at both locations — your house gets protected the whole time. We break down whatever needs it and reassemble at the destination. None of that's extra. Our guys are full-time, trained on our own program — we're not sending day labor to your house."

Pain Funnel Callbacks — This Is Where Step 1 Pays Off

If you drilled a stressor in Step 1, name it back here specifically. Generic service description is fine. Service description that names the customer's specific concern is closing.

If They Mentioned a Meaningful Piece of Furniture "I remember you mentioned the dining table was your wife's grandmother's — that's exactly the kind of piece our pad wrap and the way we strap furniture is built for. Our guys understand what irreplaceable looks like."
If They Mentioned Timing Pressure "Since your wife starts Monday, getting this done clean on Saturday matters even more — that's why we send full-time guys who've done this hundreds of times instead of a casual two-man crew. We move efficiently and we don't break things."
If They Mentioned a Past Bad Experience "You mentioned the last move was a mess — that's the experience we're built to be the opposite of. Our guys are trained on our process, they pad-wrap everything, and they treat your home like it's theirs. That's genuinely the job."
⚠️ Pitfall: Don't list service items mechanically. Match what you say to what you heard. If they mentioned a piano, lead with how you handle pianos. If you didn't drill in Step 1, Step 4 has nothing specific to anchor on.

60 seconds. Two jobs: catch errors, and get the customer saying yes. Every "correct" conditions them toward yes at the close.

Exact Script — Four Corrects "Before I pull the numbers together, let me confirm a couple quick things. You're moving the week of [date] — correct? Out of [origin], [floor / access info] — correct? Into [destination], [property type] — correct? And you're handling all the packing, so when we show up, everything in boxes is ready — we're just focused on furniture — correct?"
💡 Listen for a "well, mostly" or "sort of" instead of "correct." That's the customer flagging something. Pause and clear it up before moving on.

💡 The close doesn't start in Step 7 — it starts here. People who've been saying yes don't suddenly say no when the price comes out.

Three Rules

  • Verbal first. Always. If the customer sees the number before you've framed it, their mind locks on it and they stop listening.
  • One number. State the total — don't itemize. Itemizing invites scrutiny on every line and turns a number into a negotiation.
  • Stop talking. The pause after the number is your most powerful tool. Trust it.
Reading the Price "Based on the inventory, we're sending [X] men and [Y] truck(s). I'm estimating the job at about [Z] hours. Your total comes to $[total]. The clock starts when we arrive at your first address and stops when we finish at the destination — that's the time you're paying for."

Your tone is the tell. Quoting $400 and $6,000 should sound identical. Don't betray that one feels small and the other feels big.

Deposit Policy

  • Moves over $1,500 — 10% of the total
  • Moves $1,500 or under — flat $150
  • Both fully refundable with 48 hours notice

State the actual dollar amount when the customer asks — you'll have it from the estimate.

⚠️ Pitfall: Filling silence after the number sounds nervous. Trust the pause. The first person to speak tells you what you need to know.
The Close "To lock in your date, I'm sending the estimate over right now. At the top of the page you'll see a sign button — click that, then enter your card for the deposit. Takes about a minute and you're locked in."

No Conditional Language. Ever.

❌ Don't Say✓ Say Instead
"If you want to move forward...""To get you on the calendar..."
"If you'd like to book...""I'm sending the estimate now — sign at the top of the page."
"Would you like to put down a deposit?""Click sign at the top of the page, then enter your card."
"So, what do you think?"Silence — let the price land. Then close.

Close on the Cue

When the customer says "perfect," "that sounds great," or "okay" — take it immediately. Every word after a green light gives doubt room to re-enter.

Stay on the Phone Through Signing AND Card Entry

Walk-Through Script "I'm sending the estimate over now — you should see it in a minute or two. The sign button is at the top of the page. Click that, and once it's signed you'll be prompted to enter your card. I'll stay on with you while you walk through it."

Both steps matter. Signed without paying = no booking. Paid without signing = no booking. The moment they hang up before completing both, the booking is at risk.

For elderly or tech-challenged customers: slow down and walk them through screen by screen. If they truly can't navigate it, schedule a callback rather than letting them hang up unsigned.

If they don't say yes immediately — don't retreat. Move to Step 8. The booking is still alive.

The pattern for every objection: address the concern → reassure with something specific to Sparrow → ask for the deposit again. Always those three, in that order.

Think in Rounds — Max Two

  • Round 1: Lead with value
  • Round 2: Specific story or differentiator

Each round must give the customer something new — a new piece of information, a new angle, a real benefit. Repeating the same pitch louder is grinding. Stop after two rounds if you're not getting anywhere new.

Common Objections

ObjectionFirst Move
"I found a lower quote.""Can I ask — did they walk you through what's included? A lot of cheaper quotes don't cover disassembly, floor protection, or pad wrapping. Those aren't extras for us — they're standard. The number you're comparing to may not be the same job."
"I need to talk to my spouse.""That makes total sense. Is it more the timing, or more the investment you want to talk through?" If they tell you, address it. If both, offer to hold the date at no cost.
"I want to think about it.""Of course — what specifically do you want to think over? I want to make sure I haven't left anything unanswered." If they can't name it, ask for the deposit again.
"I want to rent my own truck.""If it's a small load and you've got friends, that can work. But the hidden costs add up fast: the rental itself, gas, potential damage, physical risk, and the time. We can usually do it for less than people expect."
Tentative Reservation — Most Underused Close "Let me hold that date for you right now at no charge — fully changeable, zero obligation. That way you have something concrete while you decide."

Any time a customer needs to think, talk to someone, or wait on a date — offer this before they hang up.

Brand-Fit Watchpoints

  • On rounds: Stop after two if you're not getting anywhere new. Every round must give something new. Repeating the same pitch louder is grinding.
  • On "make them the hero": Painting the picture of their spouse coming home to a booked move works when warm. It feels condescending when forced. If you're using it, mean it.
  • On scarcity language: Saying "I've still got my A-team available for that date" is only ethical if it's literally true. Honesty is the brand.
Full scripts for every objection in the Objection Handling guide.
💡 Energy matters as much as content. A pause followed by a confident response reads as "I've heard this before — the answer is easy." Rushing reads as defensive.

When the customer is ready, move quickly. When they're not, end the call with a specific next step.

The Rewrap — Two Sentences Max "We're going to pad-wrap everything, protect your floors and doors, and our guys are going to take care of you. This is one less thing on your plate."

Re-anchor. Don't re-sell the whole call.

The Emotional Close "It's just [deposit amount] to get you on the schedule. You can quit stressing about the move part — let's just get this done."

They don't want a moving company. They want their move to be over. Speak to that.

Quick Reference

The 10 Steps at a Glance

0 — Reset. Stand up, walk, breathe. Leave the last call there.
1 — "What has you moving today?" + "What else is going on?" Drill any stressor.
2 — Confirm what you know. Don't re-ask. Lead source: always.
3 — You name items, they confirm. Bedrooms → primary rooms → catch-all.
4 — Translate weight to crew. Proactive service description. Name their concern back.
5 — 60-second recap. Four "corrects." Yes ladder built.
6 — Verbal first. One number. Stop talking. Same tone every time.
7 — Send estimate. Sign button at top. Card entry. Both required. Stay on through both.
8 — Address → reassure → close. Two rounds max. Each round gives something new.
9 — Two-sentence rewrap. Emotional close. Always close on something.

Phrases to Memorize

"What has you moving today?"  ·  "What else is going on around the move that we should know about?"  ·  "Tell me more about that — why is that important?"  ·  "Do you have a date you're targeting, or if not, a time of month?"  ·  "Anything else we might be forgetting in there?"  ·  "Sign at the top of the page, then enter your card."  ·  "I'll stay on with you while you walk through it."  ·  "When can I follow up with you?"

Phrases to Retire

"How can I help?" → "What has you moving today?"
"If you want to book..." → "To lock in your date..."
"Would you like to put down a deposit?" → "Click sign at the top of the page, then enter your card."
"Let me know what you decide." → "I'll follow up [day] at [time]."
"So, what do you think?" → Silence.

Never Assume Their Wallet

Present every price — $350 or $6,000 — with identical confidence. Your personal budget is not relevant. The customer's inventory warrants the price.

Safety Manual

Mover Crew lead
Safety First! Every move starts and ends with safety. Read this manual completely and ask your crew lead if you have any questions.

Our Safety Commitment

Sparrow Moving is committed to maintaining a safe workplace that complies with all OSHA and DOT/FMCSA standards. Every team member is responsible for following safety procedures and reporting hazards.

Stop-Work Authority: Any crew member has the right to refuse or stop work if they identify a serious hazard that cannot be immediately corrected. Alert your supervisor before work resumes. No retaliation — ever.

Your Rights

  • You have the right to a safe workplace
  • You have the right to refuse dangerous work
  • You have the right to report injuries and hazards without retaliation
  • You have the right to access safety training and information

Pre-Job Planning

Site Walkthrough on Arrival

Before any heavy lifting starts, walk the path from the truck to the house:

  • Clear all tripping hazards — keep a lane at least 3 feet wide
  • Note floor surfaces — use floor runners on wet or slick floors
  • Check lighting; use portable lights if needed
  • Ensure pets and children are secured before starting — do not proceed until the path is clear
  • If any item looks too heavy for the current crew, get help or equipment before attempting it

Safe Lifting

Back injuries are the most common injury in the moving industry. Follow these techniques every time you lift.

Team Lifting, No Ego: If you're unsure you can lift something safely alone, ask for help — no exceptions. It's always better to take a moment than to get hurt.

Basic Lifting Stance

  1. Stand close to the load, feet shoulder-width apart
  2. Squat down — knees bent, back straight, chin slightly tucked
  3. Firm grip with your whole hand, not just fingers
  4. Core tight — lift with your legs, not your back
  5. Keep the load close to your body at waist height
  6. Turn with your feet — never twist at the waist while carrying

Two-Person Lifts

  • One person calls the lift: "Ready? Lift on three — one, two, three."
  • Communicate every step: doorways, stairs, ramp, setting down
  • For stairs: stronger person takes the lower end; use high/low carry technique
  • Never let one person walk backward without guidance
  • When setting down, coordinate timing so fingers stay clear
50 lb Rule: Anything over 50 lbs that can be dolly'd should be — don't carry it if a hand truck is available.

Common Mistakes — Avoid These

  • Twisting at the waist while carrying — this is when most back injuries happen
  • Reaching far for a heavy object — get close or get help first
  • Trying to catch a falling heavy item — step clear and let it fall
  • Pinched fingers — always communicate before setting items down
  • Overexertion — if you feel strain, set the item down and rest

Personal Protective Equipment (PPE)

Required on Every Job

  • Shoes in good condition
  • Work gloves: For rough, heavy, or sharp-edged items; grip gloves for boxes

Situational PPE

  • Hi-vis vest: Required when working near roadways or parking lots, especially at dawn/dusk
  • Safety glasses: When using power tools or in dusty environments
  • Knee pads: For extended floor-level work

Equipment

Hand Trucks & Dollies

  • Hand truck: Use for stacked boxes and items over 50 lbs whenever the environment allows. Strap tall loads. Inspect wheels before each use.
  • 4-wheel furniture dolly: Use for bulky furniture and appliances on flat surfaces. Strap items if there's any risk of sliding.

Lifting Straps

  • Forearm Forklift straps: Best for heavy but low-profile items (short dressers, mattresses). Two people loop straps under the item and lift with their forearms. Keep items low and close for tight spaces.
  • Shoulder Dollies: Best for tall or very heavy items (fridges, tall cabinets). Worn as harnesses over the shoulders — frees your hands to stabilize. Always train before using.
  • Training on both strap systems is required before use. Communicate closely — lift in unison, coordinate every turn.
Pianos & Safes: If you don't have enough crew or proper equipment, do not improvise. Reschedule or call for additional help. No exceptions.

Daily Equipment Check

Before each day, quickly inspect dollies, straps, and ramps. Any hand truck with a wobbly wheel or strap with fraying fabric gets taken out of service immediately — tag it and report it.

Housekeeping on the Job

  • Keep walkways clear at all times — minimum 3-foot lane
  • Pick up packing debris immediately — plastic wrap and tape on floors are slip hazards
  • Coil straps and cords when not in use
  • Wipe up any liquid spills immediately
  • Use floor runners on all hardwood and slick surfaces
  • Do a final sweep before leaving every job — no equipment, no debris left behind

Broken Glass Protocol

  • Never pick up glass shards with bare hands
  • Use a broom and dustpan, or thick gloves with a piece of cardboard
  • Place broken glass in a rigid container (empty box), tape it shut, and label it "BROKEN GLASS"

Vehicle Safety

See the Vehicle Operation Policy for complete details. Key points:

  • Complete pre-trip inspection before every shift
  • Use a spotter whenever backing the truck — walk-around if alone
  • Seat belts mandatory at all times
  • No phone use while driving; GPS set before moving
  • Know your truck height — posted in cab; avoid low bridges and trees
  • Heavy items go low and against the forward wall; crew lead checks all doors latched before departure

Incident Reporting

Report ALL incidents, injuries, and near-misses immediately to your crew lead. This includes:

  • Any injury, no matter how minor
  • Property damage (customer or company)
  • Vehicle accidents or damage
  • Hazards on the job site
  • Near-misses — something that almost caused injury but didn't

Near-misses are especially valuable — report them without fear of blame. They help us fix problems before someone gets hurt.

Emergency Contact: For serious injuries, call 911 first, then notify your dispatcher immediately at (612) 444-1284.

Weather Safety

Hot Weather

  • Drink water every 15–20 minutes, even if not thirsty
  • Take breaks in shade or air conditioning
  • Watch teammates for signs of heat exhaustion: heavy sweating, dizziness, nausea, rapid heartbeat
  • If someone shows symptoms — stop work, move them to shade, call 911 if it doesn't resolve quickly

Cold & Winter Weather

  • Layer clothing appropriately — Minnesota winters are serious
  • Use floor runners at all entryways; treat every surface as potentially slick
  • Keep extremities covered; take warming breaks as needed

Severe Weather (Tornado, Lightning)

  • Stop work immediately and seek shelter inside — not in the truck or under trees
  • For tornado: go to a basement or interior room of the building
  • Resume only when all-clear

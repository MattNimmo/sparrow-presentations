Specialty Item Handling

Mover Crew lead

Overview

Specialty items require extra care, specific techniques, and often additional equipment or personnel. These items are high-risk for damage and injury, but handling them well is what separates professional movers from the rest. This guide covers the most common specialty items you'll encounter.

Know Your Limits: If you haven't been trained on a specific item type, ask your crew lead before attempting to move it. Some items require specialized certifications or third-party services.

Pianos

Pianos are among the most challenging items to move. Baby and grand pianos require full disassembly and a piano board. Minimum 3–4 people. Hand tools only — no power drills.

Required Equipment

  • Piano board
  • Four-wheel dolly
  • Moving pads, shrink wrap, tape
  • Labeled parts bags
  • Socket set + Phillips screwdriver
  • Ratchet straps (dedicated strap slots on piano board)

Baby Grand / Grand — Step-by-Step

  1. Lay pads on the floor as a work surface
  2. Remove the lid: Lay 2 overlapping pads flat. Remove hinge pin with partner holding the lid. Wrap tightly
  3. Remove the music rack: Pad, roll like a burrito, secure
  4. Lid prop: If it doesn't detach, tie off with tape-string — do not leave it free (liability risk if reassembly fails)
  5. Remove the lyre (pedal assembly): Support it while unscrewing the final bolts. Diaper-wrap and label
  6. Position the piano board: Parallel to the left side, lip near the keyboard side. Lay pads across the board
  7. Always remove the left leg first (flat side). One mover unscrews while partner spots. Tip piano onto the padded board and slide until it meets the lip
  8. Remove remaining two legs. Label each leg — use 1/2/3 tape strips or L/R/bow. Mismatched legs on reassembly = tipping risk
  9. Pad-wrap each leg and place in truck
  10. Wrap piano body in pads + shrink wrap (tension to secure, do not compress)
  11. Strap piano to board using ratchet + pull-tight through dedicated strap slots
  12. Lift using rope handles; slide four-wheeler under — offset toward keyboard side (it's heavier)

Ramp Orientation — Critical

Down the ramp: Keys go first — the board lip catches forward slippage.
Up the ramp: Keys go to the back — the board lip catches backward slippage.

In the Truck

  • Tip slightly to remove the dolly
  • Strap piano flush to the truck wall
  • Nothing stacked on or leaned against the piano

Final Check Before Moving

  • All parts padded and hardware in labeled bags
  • Shrink wrap tight with no loose ends
  • Board straps evenly tensioned
  • Pinch points cleared
  • Leg labels readable
Customer Communication: Piano owners are protective. Walk them through the process before you start and handle with visible deliberateness. It builds trust.

Safes & Gun Safes

Safes range from 300 to 1,000+ lbs. Improper handling causes severe injury and property damage. Never improvise — if you don't have the right gear or crew, reschedule.

Before You Touch It

  • Confirm the safe is empty — do not ask to see the contents, just confirm it's been cleared
  • Confirm it is not bolted to the floor
  • Measure every doorway, hallway, stairway, and turn on the path

Required Equipment

  • Heavy-duty dolly rated for the safe's weight
  • 2–3 straps
  • Cardboard at strap contact points (protects safe finish — not full pad wrap)
  • Plywood or steel sheets for floor protection
  • Gloves + steel-toe boots — no exceptions

Strapping the Safe

  • Top strap: Anchors to prevent rocking
  • Center strap: Keeps it firmly in place on the dolly
  • Bottom strap (if needed): Prevents sliding
  • Place cardboard under all straps to protect the safe's surface
  • Double-check every strap before moving

Loading the Truck

  1. 2 movers push the safe up the ramp; lead mover receives and balances on the dolly
  2. Guide up the ramp with slow, controlled movement — no rushing
  3. In the truck: roll into position, unstrap, slide against the sidewall

Off the Truck

  1. 2 movers hop down and stay flatfooted to catch and guide
  2. Third mover rolls the safe down — same controlled pace

Stairs or Tricky Terrain

  • Minimum 3–4 movers
  • Use ramps, winches, or jack lifts — not brute force
  • Stair climber dolly if available for heavy safes

At the Destination

  • Use sliders or a low-profile dolly to maneuver into final position
  • Remove all wrapping
  • Check surrounding floors for damage
  • Bolt down if requested by customer
  • Clean the safe's finish before leaving
No Improvising: If you don't have enough crew or proper equipment for a safe or piano, do not attempt it. Reschedule or call for additional help. This is a firm policy — not a suggestion.

Antiques & Heirlooms

Antiques require careful handling due to age, fragility, and irreplaceable value. The emotional significance often exceeds the monetary value.

Assessment

  • Ask customer about age, value, and any fragile components
  • Look for loose joints, veneer lifting, fragile hardware
  • Document existing damage with photos before moving
  • Discuss handling concerns directly with customer

Protection Principles

  • Over-pad: More protection than you think necessary
  • No stacking: Antiques should have nothing placed on top
  • Lift, don't drag: Prevent stress on joints and legs
  • Remove components: Take off drawers, shelves, removable pieces
  • Handle last, unload first: Minimize time in truck

Common Antique Furniture

  • Clocks: Remove pendulum, secure weights, wrap mechanism separately
  • Armoires: Remove doors if possible, pad heavily
  • Marble-topped pieces: Remove and wrap marble separately
  • Chairs with arms: Wrap each arm individually
  • Writing desks: Remove and wrap legs if possible

Artwork & Mirrors

Glass and artwork require careful wrapping and handling to prevent breakage and damage to frames or surfaces.

Wrapping Process

  1. Protect surface with glassine paper or clean tissue (prevents pad texture transfer)
  2. Wrap in bubble wrap for glass pieces
  3. Cover with moving pad and secure with tape (never tape to frame)
  4. For high-value art, use custom crating or picture boxes

Transport Rules

  • Always vertical: Never lay flat—reduces pressure on glass
  • Between soft items: Place between mattresses or padded furniture
  • Face-to-face: Stack framed pieces glass-to-glass, back-to-back
  • Secure in truck: Use straps to prevent movement

Mirror Boxes

  • Use telescoping mirror boxes for large mirrors
  • Tape an X across glass surface to contain breakage
  • Mark "FRAGILE" and "THIS SIDE UP" clearly
  • Never stack anything on top of packed mirrors

Pool Tables

Pool tables must be disassembled by trained personnel. Never attempt to move assembled—the slate will crack and the frame will warp.

Disassembly Process

  1. Remove pockets and ball return components
  2. Remove rails carefully (often bolted from underneath)
  3. Remove felt only if customer wants replacement (otherwise leave attached to slate)
  4. Remove slate pieces (3-piece or single, 300-450 lbs per piece)
  5. Disassemble frame and legs

Transport

  • Slate pieces: Stand on edge, pad between pieces, secure to truck wall
  • Rails: Bundle together, wrap to protect finish
  • Frame: Disassemble and pad separately
  • Hardware: Bag and label for reassembly

Reassembly

  • Level frame precisely before placing slate
  • Use beeswax on slate seams
  • Check level after each step
  • Reassembly typically requires 2-3 hours
Certification Required: Only crew members who have completed pool table training should disassemble/reassemble these items. If needed, dispatch will assign qualified personnel.

Hot Tubs & Spas

Hot tubs are bulky, heavy, and awkward. Most require crane service or specialized equipment.

Pre-Move Preparation

  • Customer must drain and disconnect electrical 24+ hours before
  • Measure access paths carefully—fences may need removal
  • Determine if crane service is required
  • Verify destination is prepared (electrical, pad, access)

Moving Process

  • Weight range: 400-800 lbs empty
  • Use hot tub carts or sleds for transport
  • Minimum 4 people for empty tub
  • Protect exterior with pads and plastic wrap
  • Never tip more than 45 degrees (damages components)

Chandeliers & Lighting Fixtures

Delicate and often valuable, chandeliers require careful disassembly or specialized handling.

Options

  1. Customer removes: Recommend hiring an electrician for valuable fixtures
  2. We remove: Only if trained and customer approves (document existing condition)
  3. Move installed: Not recommended, but possible for short local moves with careful truck loading

If We Remove

  • Photograph before touching
  • Turn off power at breaker—verify with tester
  • Remove bulbs and bag separately
  • Remove crystals if loose (bag and label)
  • Wrap fixture heavily and place in box with packing paper
  • Mark "FRAGILE" and "THIS SIDE UP"

Large Electronics

Flat Screen TVs

Sparrow uses dedicated TV boxes with foam corners. Always upright — never flat, never weight on top.

  1. Separate the cardboard foam corners; prep each with a small piece of tape
  2. Set two bottom foam corners facing up, nested on a flat surface
  3. Remove the power cord — place it inside the box alongside the monitor
  4. Insert the TV. Push sides inward so foam hugs the screen. Thin monitors: wedge the foam divider section
  5. Tape the sides. Place top corners one at a time — tape back-to-front, over the top into the inside; add a side strip to stabilize the first corner
  6. Wrap tape around the entire box. Rubber band if still loose
  7. Always upright. Never flat. Never weight on top.
  8. Stand/legs: If they don't fit in the box, pad-wrap separately. Hardware goes in a labeled parts bag
Original box? Always ask the customer first — original packaging is ideal. Use the TV box only if they don't have it.

Computers & Servers

  • Customer should back up data before move
  • Remove and separately pack loose components
  • Wrap towers and monitors individually
  • Keep upright and away from temperature extremes

Audio Equipment

  • Original boxes preferred for high-end equipment
  • Remove turntable platters and pack separately
  • Secure speaker covers and wrap cones
  • Coil and label cables for easy reconnection

Wine Collections

Wine requires temperature control and careful handling to prevent breakage and spoilage.

  • Use wine shipping boxes with cell dividers
  • Wrap each bottle in paper
  • Store bottles on their sides (keeps corks moist)
  • Climate-controlled storage for long transit or storage
  • Load last to minimize temperature exposure
  • For large collections, refrigerated transport may be necessary

Documentation Requirements

For all specialty items:

  • ☐ Photograph item before moving (multiple angles)
  • ☐ Document any existing damage on inventory
  • ☐ Note item on high-value inventory if applicable
  • ☐ Discuss handling approach with customer
  • ☐ Photograph item after placement at destination
  • ☐ Note any issues or concerns in job notes

When to Decline

Sometimes the right answer is to recommend a specialist. Consider declining when:

  • Item exceeds crew's training or equipment capabilities
  • Risk of damage or injury is too high
  • Customer has unrealistic expectations about process
  • Access makes safe handling impossible

Consult dispatch before declining—they can arrange specialists or additional resources.

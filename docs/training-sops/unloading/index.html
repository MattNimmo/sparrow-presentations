Unloading Procedures

Mover Crew lead

The Sparrow Unloading Philosophy

Unloading is the last thing the customer watches you do — and the first thing they'll remember when they write a review. A clean, efficient unload with furniture placed correctly and a truck left in perfect condition is the difference between a 4-star job and a 5-star job. Don't lose ground at the finish line.

Never walk empty-handed. Every trip from truck to house should move something. Every trip back to the truck should return a pad, strap, or piece of equipment. Empty hands slow the job and signal low energy to the customer.

Before Unloading Begins

Walk the Destination First

  1. Walk the home with the customer — confirm where each room is and how the customer wants large items placed before a single box comes off the truck.
  2. Identify problem paths — narrow hallways, low ceilings, tight stairwells. Know your obstacles before you're carrying a dresser through them.
  3. Set up floor protection — lay neoprene floor runners and door jamb protectors at the destination just as you did at the origin. The new home needs the same protection.
  4. Position the ramp — secure it properly. A shifted ramp mid-unload causes injuries.
  5. Agree on a staging area — pick a spot near the truck door to stage items before carrying them inside. Keeps the flow moving without bottlenecks.

Crew Roles During Unload

Clear role assignment prevents everyone from doing the same task while nothing else gets done.

Standard 3-Person Unload

  • Truck person — stays in the truck, breaks down the load row by row, passes items to the ramp. Removes straps, hands off pads as items come off.
  • Ramp/relay person — receives items at the ramp, guides them inside, operates the dolly on the ramp. Keeps the flow moving between truck and house.
  • Inside person — places items in the correct rooms per customer direction. Stays ahead of incoming traffic so the path never gets blocked.
Crew lead: Communicate room assignments clearly before the first item comes off the truck. A box placed in the wrong room costs time and frustrates the customer.

Unloading Sequence

Unload in reverse order of how the truck was loaded — last in, first out.

  1. Fragile items near the door — mirrors, lamps, and artwork came off last during loading, so they come off first. Handle with care and set aside in a safe spot.
  2. Boxes — move box stacks by room label. Place in the correct room and stack neatly — don't leave random piles in the hallway.
  3. Mid-weight furniture — chairs, side tables, smaller pieces.
  4. Large upholstery — sofas, recliners. Confirm placement with customer before setting down; repositioning a couch wastes time.
  5. Heavy base items — dressers, appliances, bed frames. These come off last and go straight to their final location.
  6. Mattresses — remove bags after placement, not in the truck or hallway.

Pad Management

Removing Pads

  • Remove pads from furniture after the item is placed in its final room — not on the ramp, not in the hallway.
  • Cut stretch wrap carefully. Use a box cutter or scissors — never pull or tear, which can scratch finished wood.
  • Remove rubber bands and set aside; they're reusable.

Folding Pads — The Hot Dog / Hamburger / Hot Dog System

Pads must be folded correctly to stack cleanly and stay in good condition for the next job.

Three-Fold Method

  1. Hot dog — fold the pad lengthwise (long side to long side), bringing one half over the other.
  2. Hamburger — fold in half the short way (width-wise), bringing the bottom up to meet the top.
  3. Hot dog again — fold lengthwise one final time to produce a compact, stackable rectangle.

Seam Alignment

  • Fold with seams aligned — all seams on the same side when stacked.
  • Pads folded with seams out stack flat, stay clean, and are easier to count.
  • Stack pads on the same side of the truck they came from. Left-side pads stack left, right-side pads stack right. This keeps the truck balanced and makes the next job faster.
Count your pads. Count them at the start of the job and again when folding and stacking at the end. A missing pad means going back to look — or paying to replace it.

Reassembly at the Destination

Bed Frames

  • Retrieve the hardware bag you set aside during disassembly and confirm all hardware is present before starting.
  • Note any stripped bolts or worn hardware in the customer's presence — don't reassemble and say nothing.
  • For spindle beds, match spindles to the correct openings (they may not be interchangeable).

Dresser Mirrors

  • Reattach brackets in the correct orientation before hanging the mirror back on the dresser.
  • Have a second person hold the mirror steady while you tighten — don't prop and hope.

Dining Tables

  • Reverse the figure-eight banding before reattaching legs.
  • For glass tops: set the base first, then place the glass. Never carry glass top and base together.

Piano Legs

  • Match leg labels to the correct positions (1, 2, 3 strips marked during disassembly).
  • Mismatched legs cause tipping — this is a liability. Do not guess.
  • Right leg goes on first during reassembly.
Hardware bags: If a customer packed their own hardware, confirm they have it before disassembling anything. If hardware is missing or damaged, document it before the job ends.

End-of-Day Truck Reset

Leave the truck the way you'd want to find it at the start of a job. A reset truck is a professional truck.

Truck Reset Checklist

  • ☐ All pads folded using the hot dog/hamburger/hot dog method and stacked by side
  • ☐ Straps coiled and hung on E-track or stored in designated spot
  • ☐ Dollies secured upright against the truck wall
  • ☐ Ramp retracted and secured
  • ☐ Rubber bands collected and stored
  • ☐ Stretch wrap roll accounted for
  • ☐ Truck floor swept — no debris, dirt, or cardboard scraps
  • ☐ Truck door closed and latched
  • ☐ Pad count matches start-of-day count

Final Walk-Through

The crew lead does this with the customer before leaving the destination.

  • ☐ Walk every room — confirm all items are placed where the customer wants them
  • ☐ Check for any damage that occurred during unload — document immediately if found
  • ☐ Confirm all furniture has been reassembled
  • ☐ Remove all floor runners, door jamb protectors, and packing materials
  • ☐ Collect all pads from inside the home
  • ☐ Get customer sign-off on the Bill of Lading
  • ☐ Ask if there's anything else they need before the crew leaves
Don't rush the sign-off. A customer who feels heard at the end of a job leaves a better review. Take two minutes, do the walk-through properly.

Common Unloading Mistakes

Avoid These Errors

  • Skipping the destination walk-through — placing items in the wrong room means repositioning them later, which costs time and annoys the customer
  • Removing pads in transit — pads removed on the ramp or in the hallway create clutter and trip hazards
  • Walking empty-handed — every trip in both directions should move something
  • Leaving stretch wrap on furniture — stretch wrap left on overnight can bond to finishes, especially in heat
  • Skipping the pad count — missing pads discovered after leaving the job are rarely recovered
  • Rushing reassembly — stripped bolts or mismatched legs discovered after the crew leaves become damage claims
  • Skipping the truck reset — a dirty, disorganized truck slows the next job and reflects on the whole crew

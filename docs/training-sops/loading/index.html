Loading Procedures

Mover Crew lead

The Sparrow Loading Philosophy

A well-loaded truck is the foundation of a successful move. Proper loading protects the customer's belongings, maximizes space efficiency, ensures safe transport, and makes unloading faster and easier. At Sparrow, we take pride in our loading quality—it's one of the things that sets us apart.

Safety First: Never compromise safety for speed. A few extra minutes loading correctly prevents damage, injuries, and customer complaints. If you're unsure about placement, ask your crew lead.

Truck Preparation

Before Loading Begins

  1. Inspect the truck – Check floor for debris, moisture, or damage. Sweep if needed.
  2. Verify equipment – Ensure you have adequate pads, straps, dollies, and tie-downs for the job size.
  3. Set up the deck – Position the ramp securely. If using a lift gate, verify operation.
  4. Organize pad staging – Stack clean pads inside truck door for easy access during loading.
  5. Review the inventory – Know what you're loading before you start. Identify heavy items and specialty pieces.

Loading Sequence

The order you load items matters. Follow this general sequence for optimal results:

Loading Order (Front to Back)

  1. Heavy base items – Appliances, dressers, filing cabinets (against front wall)
  2. Mattresses & box springs – Stand on edge against side walls
  3. Sofas & large upholstery – On end when possible, padded
  4. Tables & desks – Inverted on top of base layer when possible
  5. Boxes – Heaviest on bottom, lighter on top, fill gaps
  6. Fragile & light items – Lamps, artwork, mirrors loaded last
  7. Customer essentials – Items needed first at destination near door

Building the Load

Tier System

Think of the truck in three tiers:

  • Base tier (floor to 4 feet) – Heavy, sturdy items that form a stable foundation
  • Middle tier (4-7 feet) – Medium-weight items, boxes, smaller furniture
  • Top tier (7 feet to ceiling) – Light items only: pillows, lamp shades, light boxes

Wall-to-Wall Loading

Build each row completely before moving to the next:

  • Start at the front wall and work toward the door
  • Fill each row wall-to-wall before starting the next
  • Use boxes and small items to fill gaps—no wasted space
  • Each row should support itself without relying on items behind it

Weight Distribution

  • Distribute heavy items evenly left-to-right
  • Keep the majority of weight forward of the rear axle
  • Never load one side significantly heavier than the other
  • Heavy items should never be stacked on fragile items

Padding & Protection

What Gets Padded

  • All wood furniture surfaces (dressers, tables, headboards, desks)
  • All leather and upholstered items
  • Appliances with finished exteriors
  • Mirrors and artwork (fully wrapped)
  • Electronics and monitors
  • Any surface that could be scratched by contact with other items

Padding Techniques

  • Full wrap – Cover entire piece for high-value items
  • Surface pads – Drape pads over exposed surfaces
  • Edge protection – Wrap corners and edges prone to damage
  • Buffer pads – Place between items that will touch during transport
Pad Management: Keep track of your pads. Count them at the start of the job and count them again at the end. Lost pads cost money and slow down future jobs.

Securing the Load

Strap Placement

  • Use at least 2-3 straps per loaded row
  • Straps should be tight but not crushing items
  • Anchor to side rails—never wrap around single items
  • Tighten after loading each section, not just at the end

E-Track System

Our trucks are equipped with E-track rails. Use them properly:

  • Insert E-track fittings at appropriate intervals
  • Distribute strap points evenly along the load
  • Check that fittings are fully locked before tensioning
  • Use ratchet straps for heavy sections

Signs of Improper Securing

  • Items shift when ramp is raised
  • Visible gaps where items could fall into
  • Top-heavy sections that could topple
  • Loose straps with visible slack

Special Loading Situations

Mattresses

  • Always use mattress bags—no exceptions
  • Stand on edge, never flat (takes too much space)
  • Place against wall with items in front to prevent falling
  • King mattresses should go in first, against front wall

Mirrors & Glass

  • Wrap in paper, then pad, then secure
  • Load vertically, never flat
  • Place between mattresses or padded items when possible
  • Mark as fragile and communicate to team

Appliances

  • Refrigerators: Remove shelves, tape doors, keep upright
  • Washers: Secure drum with transit bolts when available
  • Dryers: Tape door closed, disconnect vent hose
  • Load appliances against walls, strapped independently

Long Items

  • Rugs: Roll tightly, secure with tape, load last on top
  • Floor lamps: Disassemble when possible, pad shades separately
  • Curtain rods: Bundle together, wrap ends
  • Lumber/boards: Secure to prevent shifting

Box Loading

Box Stacking Rules

  • Weight order: Heavy on bottom, medium middle, light on top
  • Same sizes together: Stack matching box sizes for stability
  • Fragile boxes: Load last, near door, with buffer around them
  • Maximum height: Never stack past 7 feet or truck ceiling
  • Labels visible: Face labels out for easy identification at destination

Using Boxes as Fill

Boxes are your best tool for eliminating gaps:

  • Keep a variety of box sizes accessible during loading
  • Fill voids as you build each row
  • Small boxes and odd shapes go in gaps between furniture
  • A tightly packed load doesn't shift

Final Load Check

Before closing the truck door, verify:

  • ☐ All items padded and protected appropriately
  • ☐ Load secured with adequate straps (no visible movement)
  • ☐ No items blocking the door that could fall out
  • ☐ Weight balanced left-to-right
  • ☐ Fragile items marked and communicated to driver
  • ☐ Customer essentials accessible near door
  • ☐ Pad count matches expectations
  • ☐ Ramp and equipment stowed properly

Common Loading Mistakes

Avoid These Errors

  • Rushing – Speed without care leads to damage and injuries
  • Top-heavy stacking – Heavy items high in the load can shift and crush items below
  • Insufficient padding – Every item that can scratch needs protection
  • Leaving gaps – Open spaces allow items to shift during transport
  • Over-strapping – Too-tight straps can damage furniture
  • Loading fragile items early – They should be last in, first out
  • Ignoring weight distribution – Unbalanced loads affect vehicle handling
